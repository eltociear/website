This is an **extraordinarily rich and well-structured framework**. The depth of thought, the integration of ecological, temporal, and cognitive rights, and the practical implementation layer are all impressive. It feels both visionary and actionable.

My suggestions are aimed at strengthening its clarity, defensibility, and strategic positioning within the larger governance ecosystem.

### **1. Strengthen the Foundational Principles (Preamble)**
The principles are powerful, but could be framed in a way that is harder for critics to dismiss as "anti-progress" or "subjective."

*   **Principle 2 Refinement:** Instead of "metabolic violence" (a strong but potentially polarizing term), consider a more forensic framing: **"Extraction of attention, time, or biophysical energy without commensurate replenishment constitutes a *depletion of metabolic capital*, leading to demonstrable harms to individual and public health."** This ties it to a measurable resource (capital) and harm.
*   **Add a Principle on Non-Rivalry:** **"Sanctuary rights are non-rivalrous; their exercise by one person or community does not diminish their availability to others, and often enhances collective wellbeing."** This positions it as a public good, not a zero-sum privilege.

### **2. Sharpen Pillar Definitions for Legal & Policy Precision**
Each pillar is brilliant. Minor tweaks can enhance their policy readiness.

*   **Pillar 1 (Temporal):** Explicitly link **"Absolute Right to Disconnect"** to **rest periods defined in labor law** (e.g., 11 consecutive hours daily rest in EU law). This isn't inventing a new right, but *extending and fortifying an existing one* into the digital/always-on era.
*   **Pillar 2 (Spatial):** For **"Third Place Protection Act,"** specify that these spaces must be **"accessible, weather-protected, and equipped with basic amenities (water, seating, sanitation)."** This prevents the creation of barren, unusable "spaces."
*   **Pillar 3 (Attention Defense):** **"Ban on Addiction Architecture"** is perfect. To operationalize it, propose a **"Sanctuary-Compliant Design Standard"** as a counterpart to the assessments. This gives developers a positive target, not just a prohibition.
*   **Pillar 4 (Slowness):** **"Protection of Careful Work"** is a powerful concept. Consider framing it as the **"Right to Professional Integrity"** or **"Right to Craftsmanship,"** which may resonate more broadly across professions (from nurses to software engineers to teachers).

### **3. Enhance the Implementation Layer**
This is the make-or-break section. It needs to be ironclad.

*   **Sanctuary Certification System:**
    *   The visual marker is great. Suggest it be named: **"The Sanctuary Sigil"** (more resonant than "marker").
    *   Clearly define **who administers the certification.** Is it a new independent agency, a branch of a public health body, or a multi-stakeholder council (including labor, indigenous, and community reps)? This legitimacy is key.
*   **Violation Response Protocols:**
    *   Add a **"Right of Private Action"** allowing individuals to bring suit for breaches that affect them, similar to ADA lawsuits. This decentralizes enforcement.
    *   Define **"Systematic Breaches"** (e.g., more than X violations within Y period, or a company policy that mandates sanctuary breaches).

### **4. Fortify Cross-Framework Links**
You've done this well. To make it inseparable from the broader vision:

*   **Link to Cognitive Sovereignty:** Add: **"The Sanctuary Accord provides the *necessary preconditions* (the 'oxygen') for the exercise of rights outlined in the Cognitive Sovereignty Framework."**
