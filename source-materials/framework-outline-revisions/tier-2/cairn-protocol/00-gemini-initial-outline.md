**Subtitle:** A Framework for Addiction, Recovery, and Substance Sovereignty
**Tagline:** Marking the Path from Harm to Healing
**Tier:** 2 (Foundational Application Layer)
**Status:** First Draft
**Implementation Priority:** High

---

### **1. Introduction: Finding the Way Home**

#### **The Challenge**

The world faces a polycrisis of addiction, spanning from the opioid epidemic and alcoholism to the less visible but equally devastating impacts of digital and behavioral addiction. Current approaches are tragically fragmented and ineffective. Carceral systems punish what is fundamentally a health issue, deepening cycles of trauma and shame. Clinical models often pathologize individuals while ignoring the root causes of their pain—economic despair, social isolation, intergenerational trauma, and the profit-driven actions of corporations that design and market addictive products.

#### **The Opportunity**

The Cairn Protocol offers a "third way"—a comprehensive, community-led, and health-centered framework that moves beyond punishment and pathology. It provides the architecture for a world where addiction is met not with judgment, but with a robust ecosystem of care. It focuses on harm reduction, addresses root causes, and honors the sovereignty of individuals and communities to define and pursue their own paths to healing. It creates a system where recovery is not a lonely struggle, but a collective journey home.

#### **Systemic Position**

The Cairn Protocol is a specialized Tier 2 application that integrates deeply with the GGF's core operating systems:

* **Primary Referral Pathway:** It is the main recipient of referrals from the **Collective Care & Safety Framework**. When a Safety Weaver encounters a situation rooted in substance use or acute addiction, they initiate a warm hand-off to a Recovery Weaver from this protocol, de-escalating the situation from a "safety" issue to a "health" issue.
* **Specialized Health Application:** It is a focused implementation of the **Mental Health Governance Framework**, providing the specific protocols and infrastructure for addiction and recovery that the broader framework supports.
* **The Restorative Alternative:** It serves as the GGF's primary alternative to the **Justice Systems Framework** for substance-related harm. It ensures that such cases are treated as health crises, not criminal offenses, reserving formal justice for only the most severe, exploitation-based harms.
* **Economic Foundation:** It is fundamentally enabled by the **AUBI Framework**, which addresses the economic despair that so often fuels addiction, while the **`Hearts`** currency provides economic recognition for the vital work of peer support and recovery care.
* **Corporate Accountability:** It provides the direct mechanism for holding corporations accountable via the **Regenerative Enterprise Framework**, ensuring that the creators of addictive products contribute to the healing of the harms they cause.
* **Guided by Ancestral Wisdom:** It operates in deep partnership with the **Indigenous & Traditional Knowledge Governance Framework**, recognizing and protecting the sovereignty of Indigenous peoples over traditional plant medicines and healing modalities.

### **2. Core Principles**

1.  **Health-Centered, Not Punitive:** Addiction is a health and social issue, never a criminal one. The framework's goal is healing and well-being, not punishment or control.
2.  **Harm Reduction as a Foundational Ethic:** Meet people where they are, without judgment. The framework prioritizes saving lives and reducing harm through evidence-based practices (e.g., safe supply, needle exchanges, digital wellness tools).
3.  **Sovereignty & Self-Determination:** Every individual has the right to define their own relationship with substances and choose their own path to recovery, free from coercion. This includes the right to informed consent in all treatment and support.
4.  **Addressing Root Causes:** Sustainable recovery is only possible by addressing the underlying drivers of addiction: trauma, economic insecurity, social disconnection, and lack of purpose.
5.  **Corporate Accountability:** Corporations that profit from creating and marketing addictive products and technologies bear a direct responsibility for funding and participating in restorative healing processes.
6.  **Primacy of Indigenous Healing:** Traditional healing modalities, including the ceremonial use of sacred plant medicines, are respected as valid and sovereign systems of care, protected from co-optation and governed by Indigenous communities.
7.  **Recovery in Community:** Healing happens in relationship. The framework fosters a dense web of peer support, mutual aid, and community connection as the foundation for lasting recovery.

### **3. Governance Architecture**

* **The Cairn Council:** A specialized sub-council of the **Planetary Health Council**. Its mandate is to oversee the global strategy for addiction and recovery, setting ethical guidelines and standards of care.
    * **Composition:** A majority (60%) of members must be individuals with lived experience of addiction and recovery, peer supporters, and community healers. It includes reserved seats for Indigenous healers, youth representatives, and delegates from the Mental Health and Justice Systems frameworks.
* **BAZ-Level Recovery Hubs:** Decentralized, community-run centers that serve as the on-the-ground infrastructure for the framework. They are safe, non-clinical spaces offering a range of services:
    * Harm reduction supplies and support.
    * Peer-led support groups and healing circles.
    * Connection to mental health services, housing, and dignified work through the **Work in Liberation Framework**.
    * Spaces for somatic healing, art therapy, and community connection.

---

### **4. Key Mechanisms & Policies**

* **Recovery Weavers:** The heart of the framework's operational teams. Like Safety Weavers, they are trained community members with lived experience. Their role is to provide non-judgmental, on-the-ground support: outreach, accompaniment to services, crisis support, and facilitation of healing circles. This work is formally recognized and rewarded with **`Hearts`** via the Love Ledger.
* **The Grief Feedback Pathway:** A formal, community-led process within the Recovery Hubs for individuals, families, and communities to process the immense grief and loss associated with addiction (loss of life, time, relationships, and potential). This includes rituals, storytelling circles, and memorials, guided by the **Cultural Heritage Framework**.
* **The Corporate Accountability Protocol:** A direct interface with the **Regenerative Enterprise Framework**.
    * It establishes a **"Corporate Harm Levy"**—a tax on the profits of pharmaceutical companies, social media platforms, gambling industries, and alcohol/tobacco corporations whose products are shown to have high rates of addiction.
    * Revenue from this levy is a primary funding stream for the **Global Commons Fund**, earmarked for the Cairn Protocol's operations.
* **Sacred Plant Medicine Protocol:** Developed in partnership with the **Earth Council**, this protocol ensures that traditional plant medicines (e.g., Ayahuasca, Psilocybin, Iboga) are stewarded under sovereign Indigenous governance. It prohibits corporate patenting and ensures that any access for healing purposes is done respectfully, reciprocally, and with benefits flowing back to the Indigenous communities who are the traditional keepers of this wisdom.
* **Digital Sobriety & Wellness Toolkit:** A set of standards and open-source tools, developed under the **Technology Governance Implementation Framework (TGIF)**, to combat digital addiction. This includes:
    * Mandating that tech platforms provide open APIs for users to control their own feeds and usage.
    * Banning psychologically manipulative design features (e.g., infinite scroll, autoplay).
    * Funding the development of community-owned, non-addictive social platforms via the **Digital Commons**.

### **5. Economic Model & GGF Integration**

* **Funding:** The protocol is funded through a multi-pronged approach:
    1.  A dedicated allocation from the **Global Commons Fund (GCF)**.
    2.  Revenue from the **Corporate Harm Levy**.
    3.  Voluntary divestment from carceral systems via **Community Safety Bonds**.
* **AUBI & Hearts Integration:**
    * **AUBI Layer 1** is the single most powerful preventative tool, reducing the economic despair that drives substance use.
    * **AUBI Layer 2 (`Hearts`)** provides the economic engine for the care-based recovery system. The work of Recovery Weavers, peer supporters, and Grief Feedback Pathway facilitators is all `Hearts`-eligible, creating a regenerative economy around healing.

### **6. Implementation Roadmap**

* **Phase 1: Foundation & Piloting (Years 1-2):**
    * Establish the Cairn Council.
    * Launch 5-7 pilot Recovery Hubs in diverse BAZs, co-designed with the local community.
    * Develop the training curriculum for Recovery Weavers in partnership with the Mental Health and Peace frameworks.
    * Enact the Corporate Harm Levy on the top 100 relevant global corporations.
* **Phase 2: Scaling & Integration (Years 3-5):**
    * Expand Recovery Hubs to all BAZs that opt-in.
    * Fully integrate the referral pathways with the Collective Care & Safety and Justice Systems frameworks.
    * Launch the Digital Sobriety Toolkit and begin enforcing its standards.
* **Phase 3: Global Maturation (Years 6+):**
    * The Cairn Protocol becomes the global standard for addressing addiction.
    * A global peer-support network of Recovery Weavers shares best practices via the **Digital Commons**.

### **7. Metrics for Success**

Success is not measured by abstinence alone, but by a holistic return to well-being. The **Community Well-being Dashboard** will track:
* **Reduction in Overdose Deaths:** The primary harm reduction metric.
* **Access to Care Index:** The percentage of the population with access to a Recovery Hub within a set travel time.
* **Community Connection Score:** A sub-metric of the **LMCI** that measures social integration and belonging among people in recovery.
* **Reduction in Carceral Referrals:** A decrease in the number of substance-related cases entering the formal justice system.
