## The Five Trojan Horse Techniques

### Technique 1: Speak Their Metrics, Track Yours

**The method:** Report success using their KPIs while privately measuring what you care about.

**Example: Council sanctuary pilot**

**Their metrics (what you report):**
- Cost per participant
- Service utilization rates
- Citizen satisfaction scores
- Partnership agreements signed

**Your metrics (what you track privately):**
- Community governance capacity
- Hours of unpaid care work enabled
- Number of people refusing exploitative work
- Network density among sanctuary participants

**Why this works:** They see "successful program." You see "organizing infrastructure."

**The discipline:** Track both sets rigorously. Never lie about theirs (maintains trust), but focus your real strategy on yours.

**The Dual Dashboard Principle:**

Think of it like running two operating systems simultaneously:

**Dashboard A (For the Boss):**
- ROI, efficiency gains, stakeholder satisfaction
- Service delivery metrics, cost savings
- Keeps you employed, funded, credible

**Dashboard B (For the Movement):**
- Power shifts, democratic capacity built
- Wealth redirected, sovereignty gained
- Keeps you honest, strategic, effective

**The warning signs:**
- **If you only have Dashboard A:** You've been captured. You're just a good employee now.
- **If you only have Dashboard B:** You'll be fired. You're not translating effectively.
- **The Architect runs both:** Reports A to institution, strategizes from B with movement.

**Every 6 months, audit yourself:** Are both dashboards still tracking? Are they diverging (good) or converging (capture)?

**Real UK example: Bristol One City Plan**
- **Their metric:** "Jobs created in green sectors" (reported in council documents)
- **Your metric:** "Number of workers in cooperatives, hours reduced via AUBI pilot, community-controlled assets" (tracked privately, shared in movement)
- **Result:** City sees economic development. Movement sees cooperative infrastructure.

### The Corporate Immune System: Why Mimicry Matters

**Here's what they don't teach you in activist school:**

**Institutions have immune systems.** Just like your body attacks foreign cells, institutions attack foreign ideas.

**The immune response:**
- HR flags you as "not a culture fit"
- Compliance blocks your proposals as "too risky"
- Middle management says "that's not how we do things here"
- You're sidelined, isolated, eventually pushed out

**The mimicry strategy:** You have to look like a native protein so you can deliver the payload without triggering inflammation.

**What mimicry looks like:**
- Use their slide deck templates
- Learn their acronyms and buzzwords
- Dress like they dress (if they wear suits, you wear suits)
- Reference their heroes (not Chomsky, cite McKinsey)
- Show up to their rituals (away days, team drinks, company events)

**Why it works:** The immune system scans for "one of us" vs. "foreign body." If you signal "one of us" consistently, you can introduce structural changes they'd normally reject.

**The line:** Mimicry is about form (language, appearance, behavior), not substance (values, goals, strategy). You're not becoming them. You're wearing their skin.

**Example:**
- **Bad:** Show up to council meeting in anarchist hoodie, demand abolition of private property
- **Good:** Show up in business casual, propose "community asset management innovation pilot"
- **Same goal:** Community land trusts removing property from speculation
- **Different immune response:** First gets you labeled "troublemaker." Second gets you "innovative thinker."

**The discipline:** You must be fluent in their performance (so you're not rejected) while maintaining your analysis (so you're not captured).

**The method:** Every institution has people frustrated with the status quo. Identify and support them.

**How to find them:**

### Technique 3: Use Their Crisis, Offer Your Solution

**The method:** Institutions in crisis become flexible. Your frameworks solve their immediate problem while advancing your agenda.

**Current UK crises:**

**Why this works:** In crisis, "We've tried everything, might as well try this" overcomes normal resistance.

**Example:** Council facing climate litigation (not meeting carbon targets). Arrive with ecological sanctuary proposal that provides carbon capture, biodiversity, AND addresses housing/community needs. Frame as "nature-based solution to multiple crises." Council desperate, approves.

## The Ethical Guardrails: When to Infiltrate vs. When to Build Parallel

**Infiltration isn't always the answer.** Sometimes building parallel is better strategy.

### Infiltrate When:

‚úì **Institution is reformable**
- Has progressive elements inside
- Facing crisis making them flexible
- Your framework solves their actual problems
- You can maintain integrity while inside

‚úì **Resources/legitimacy worth the effort**
- Controls significant budget or permissions
- Provides credentials/legitimacy useful to movement
- Offers platform for scaling your work
- Access to networks difficult to build externally

‚úì **You can avoid capture**
- Have external support network (not dependent on institution)
- Clear boundaries on what you won't compromise
- Exit strategy if institution tries to neutralize you
- Track your metrics, not just theirs

### Build Parallel When:

‚úó **Institution is captured beyond reform**
- Completely controlled by oligarchy interests
- No progressive elements, all resistance crushed
- Your participation would legitimize their facade
- Working inside would burn your credibility

‚úó **Time/energy better spent elsewhere**
- Infiltration would consume capacity needed for building
- Parallel structures already viable
- Institution's legitimacy declining anyway
- Your movement doesn't need their validation

‚úó **Capture risk too high**
- Institution has sophisticated co-option mechanisms
- Your financial survival would depend on them
- No external network to keep you accountable
- History shows they neutralize infiltrators

### Case Study 1: The Preston Model (UK) ‚Äî TACTICAL BREAKDOWN

**Context:** Preston council facing economic decline, austerity cuts, growing inequality (2011-2013).

- Framed as "community wealth building" (sounds innovative, not radical)

**The tactical genius:**

**Phase 1: The Boring Entry Point (2011-2013)**
- Started with procurement‚Äîliterally the most boring thing in local government
- No one pays attention to procurement. It's just "how councils buy stuff."
- This meant: low resistance, technical not ideological, officers willing to pilot

**Phase 2: The Evidence Base (2013-2015)**
- Documented that ¬£750M+ was "leaking" out of Preston economy annually
- Showed councils and NHS buying from London/multinational corporations
- Framed redirecting spending as "economic development" (not redistribution)
- Built case using hard numbers (multiplier effects, local jobs created)

**Phase 3: The Implementation (2015-2017)**
- Created "anchor institutions" network (council, NHS, university, housing, police)
- Each committed to redirect % of spending to local/cooperative businesses
- Supported cooperative development (training, loans, procurement preferences)
- Started community wealth building infrastructure (cooperative bank, land trusts)

**Phase 4: The Upload (2017-present)**
- What started as procurement became structural transformation
- Cooperatives own productive assets (not just services, but manufacturing, property)
- Community land trusts remove land from speculation
- Participatory budgeting gives residents decision power
- Democratic ownership patterns institutionalized

**The resistance they faced:**

**Early (2013-2014):**
- Local business groups warned against "municipal socialism"
- Council officers said "this isn't how procurement works"
- Some councillors worried about electoral backlash

**How they overcame early resistance:**
- Used economic arguments (not ideological): "This keeps money local, creates jobs"
- Showed international precedents (Cleveland Model in US, Mondrag√≥n in Spain)
- Started small (5% redirect target, scaled up)
- Built coalition including business (emphasized "local" not "cooperative")

**Medium (2015-2017):**
- National media tried to portray as "hard left" (Telegraph, Daily Mail attacks)
- Central government (Conservative) attempted regulatory blocks
- Some cooperative businesses struggled (capacity, quality issues)

**How they overcame medium resistance:**
- Documented success meticulously (employment data, business creation, poverty reduction)
- Got legitimacy from respected institutions (RSA, New Economics Foundation)
- Fixed cooperative capacity issues (training, mentoring, shared services)
- Kept messaging economic not ideological

**Late (2017-present):**
- Success created visibility, increased scrutiny
- Risk of model being watered down when replicated elsewhere
- Tension between scaling (needs resources) and maintaining democratic control

**Current status:**
- Preston measurably more resilient than comparable cities
- Model replicated in 30+ UK councils (various levels of fidelity)
- Created national legitimacy for community wealth building
- Demonstrated that procurement law can rewire ownership patterns

**The lesson:**

**The most boring part of local government (procurement) became the entry point for structural transformation.**

**The translation:** 
- Say "supporting local economy" 
- Mean "building democratic ownership of productive assets"
- Use "community wealth building"

**By the time anyone realized what had happened, it was too late.** The cooperatives existed. The community land trusts were legal entities. The procurement redirects were policy. The parallel economy was functioning.
