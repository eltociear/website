### Day 1 (Monday): The Attention Audit

**What you're doing:** Taking inventory of where your attention actually goes.

**Why it matters:** You can't redirect what you don't measure. Most people have no idea how fragmented their attention is.

**The practice:**

**Treat this like geoguessr for your own mind.** You're not trying to change the location yet—you're just identifying the meta. "Ah, scrolling Twitter at 8am is the despair-loop biome." Spotting the pattern is 90% of the game.

1. **Install a time-tracking app** (RescueTime, ActivityWatch, Toggl Track, or just manual logging)
   - Track everything for one full day
   - Don't change behavior—just observe

2. **At the end of the day, categorize:**
   - **Deep work** (sustained focus on meaningful tasks)
   - **Shallow work** (necessary but not cognitively demanding)
   - **Reactive work** (responding to others' priorities)
   - **Passive consumption** (scrolling, watching, reading without purpose)
   - **Active rest** (genuine recovery—walk, sleep, physical activity)

3. **Calculate your ratios:**
   - What % is deep work?
   - What % is reactive?
   - What % is passive consumption?

**Most people discover: `<`5% deep work, 40%+ reactive, 30%+ passive consumption.**

**You can't build anything strategic from that foundation.**

**The insight:** Your attention is the only non-renewable resource you have. Where it goes determines what becomes possible.

**Tomorrow's target:** You'll start reclaiming it.

### Day 2 (Tuesday): The Notification Purge

**What you're doing:** Eliminating attention hijacking at the source.

**Why it matters:** Every notification is an interruption designed to fragment your focus. You can't think systemically while getting pulled into everyone else's urgency.

**The practice:**

1. **Turn off ALL notifications except:**
   - Actual humans calling you (phone calls only)
   - Critical systems (security alerts, actual emergencies)
   
   That means:
   - No email notifications
   - No app notifications
   - No badge counters
   - No social media pings
   - No news alerts

2. **Set specific check-in times:**
   - Email: 3x per day maximum (morning, midday, evening)
   - Social media: 1x per day if needed for work, otherwise delete apps from phone
   - News: 1x per day, from sources you choose deliberately

3. **Create a "deep work block":**
   - Pick your most alert 2-hour window (usually morning)
   - Phone on Do Not Disturb
   - Email closed
   - Browser closed except tabs needed for current task
   - **Just work**

**What you'll notice:**

First hour: Phantom vibrations, anxiety, compulsive reach for phone
First day: Surprising how much you get done
First week: Realization of how much mental space you've reclaimed

**The resistance:**

"But what if there's an emergency?"
→ If it's actually an emergency, they'll call. Everything else can wait 2 hours.

"But my job requires immediate responses!"
→ Test it. Most "urgent" things aren't. Set expectations: "I check email at 9am, 1pm, 5pm."

**Tomorrow's target:** You'll use that reclaimed attention for something that compounds.

### Day 3 (Wednesday): The Contemplative Minimum Viable Practice

**What you're doing:** Building the capacity for presence you learned in Chapter 8.

**Why it matters:** Without nervous system regulation, you'll default to reactive patterns under pressure. This is tactical, not spiritual.

**The practice: 10 minutes, non-negotiable.**

**Morning routine (7 minutes):**
1. Wake up
2. Before phone, before email, before anything
3. Sit somewhere quiet
4. Box breathing: 4 cycles (about 1 minute)
5. Zone check: "What zone am I starting this day in?" (30 seconds)
6. Set one intention: "Today I will maintain green zone during [specific situation]" (30 seconds)
7. 5 minutes silent sitting
   - Not trying to empty your mind
   - Not trying to achieve anything
   - Just sitting with whatever arises
   - When you notice you're thinking, return attention to breath
   - Repeat 100 times

**Evening bookend (3 minutes):**
1. Before bed
2. Recall: "What zone was I in most today?"
3. Notice: "When did I catch yellow zone and regulate?"
4. Plan: "Tomorrow I'll apply the pause button when [specific trigger]"

**That's it. 10 minutes total.**

**Not meditation as spiritual practice. This is nervous system training as strategic capacity.**

**What you'll notice:**

Week 1: Feels awkward, thoughts everywhere, "I'm bad at this"
Week 2: Occasional moments of actual quiet
Week 3: Starting to catch yellow zone in real time
Week 4: Measurably better decisions under pressure

**The resistance:**

"I don't have 10 minutes."
→ You have 2+ hours of passive consumption daily (Day 1 audit). You have time.

"My mind is too busy."
→ Everyone's is. That's not failure, that's the starting condition. You're training attention.

"This feels weird/woo-woo."
→ Frame it differently: You're doing nervous system drills. Athletes train their bodies, you're training your regulation capacity.

**Tomorrow's target:** You'll connect this internal work to external organizing.

### Day 4 (Thursday): Discord → Real World

**What you're doing:** Converting one online community connection into real-world organizing.

**Why it matters:** The Movement's strength (community) + the Extractors' strength (building things) = infrastructure that scales. But it starts with real humans in real proximity.

**The practice:**

1. **Identify your online communities:**
   - Discord servers you're active in
   - Reddit communities you engage with
   - Twitter mutuals you actually interact with
   - Group chats that aren't just memes

2. **Pick ONE person** from ONE community who:
   - Lives within 50 miles of you
   - Shares strategic interests (not just vibes)
   - You've interacted with at least 3 times

3. **Reach out directly:**
   
   **The script:**
   
   "Hey [name], I've been thinking about moving some of this online energy into actual local organizing. Want to meet for coffee and talk about what that could look like in [your city]? No agenda, just exploration."

4. **Meet in person.**
   - Pick a café or public space
   - Bring a notebook
   - Ask: "What would you build here if you had resources?"
   - Listen for overlap between their answer and your emerging strategy
   - End with: "Let's do this again in 2 weeks. I'll invite one other person, you invite one."

**What you're building:**

Not a movement (too soon).
Not an organization (too formal).
**A tactical pod**—3-5 people who can think together, plan together, act together.

**Critical distinction: A pod is not a friend group.**

You don't have to like the same music or have deep emotional processing sessions. You just need shared **competence** and shared **intent**. 

You're building a raid team, not a book club. You need people who can:
- Show up when they say they will
- Think strategically under pressure
- Execute tasks independently
- Coordinate without constant hand-holding

Friendship might develop. But that's a bonus, not the requirement.

**The math:**

You + 1 person = conversation
You + 2 people = possibility
You + 4 people = pod
3 pods = network
5 networks = you've changed your city

**But it starts with coffee.**

**The resistance:**

"Online organizing is more efficient."
→ For coordination, yes. For trust-building and actual execution, no. You need both.

"What if they say no?"
→ Try another person. You're looking for the 1 in 10 who's ready, not convincing the 9 who aren't.

"What if we meet and have nothing to say?"
→ Then you learned they're not your pod. That's data, not failure.

**Tomorrow's target:** You'll start tracking how power actually moves.

### Day 5 (Friday): The Power Map

**What you're doing:** Starting a pattern log of where power actually flows in your city.

**Why it matters:** You learned systems thinking in Chapter 6. Now you apply it to your actual environment. Most activists don't know who owns what, who's connected to whom, or where leverage points are.

**The practice:**

1. **Create a simple spreadsheet or notebook** with these columns:
   - Actor (person/org/institution)
   - Power source (money, votes, land, permits, information, violence)
   - Connections (who do they work with/for/against)
   - Interests (what do they actually want)
   - Leverage point (what could influence them)

2. **Start with ONE issue you care about** (housing, climate, education, healthcare)

3. **Map the flows, not just the titles:**
   
   Don't just ask "Who has influence?"
   
   Ask: **"Where are the flows?"**
   - Where does the **money** flow in your city? (Follow tax dollars, developer payments, campaign contributions)
   - Where does **attention** flow? (Which media outlets, influencers, community groups shape narrative)
   - Where does **permission** flow? (Who sits at the valve that opens/closes projects)
   - Where does **information** flow? (Who knows things before others, who controls data)

   **Systems thinking applied:** Power isn't just who has titles. It's who controls the flows that make things happen or prevent them from happening.

4. **Example: Housing**

   | Actor | Power Source | Connections | Interests | Leverage Point |
   |-------|-------------|-------------|-----------|----------------|
   | City Council | Votes, zoning authority | Developer donors, homeowner groups | Re-election, developer money | Public pressure, alternative funding |
   | Mega Developer Corp | Capital, land ownership | Council members, banks | ROI, property values | Permit delays, public opposition |
   | Tenant Union | Numbers, disruption capacity | Community groups, lawyers | Rent stabilization | Coalition with homeowners on property tax |
   | Planning Commission | Technical authority | Council appointees | "Professional" reputation | Data showing current policy failure |

**Spend 30 minutes on this.**

Don't try to map everything. Just start seeing the actual structure instead of abstract "the system."

**What you'll notice:**

- Power is more fragmented than you thought (opportunity)
- Same names appear across multiple domains (chokepoints)
- Supposed enemies have overlapping interests (coalition potential)
- You've been pushing on powerless targets

**The insight:** Most organizing fails because it applies pressure to people who can't or won't change things. The power map shows you where actual leverage is.

**Tomorrow's target:** You'll build this into a sustainable practice.

### Day 6 (Saturday): The Weekly Strategy Review

**What you're doing:** Creating a recurring structure for reflection and course-correction.

**Why it matters:** Without regular review, you'll drift back to reactive patterns. This is your OODA loop for personal strategy.

**The practice: 30 minutes every Saturday morning**

**Review last week (15 minutes):**

1. **Attention audit:**
   - Where did my deep work time actually go?
   - What hijacked my focus?
   - What would I do differently?

2. **Regulation check:**
   - How many times did I catch yellow zone before redlining?
   - What were my main triggers?
   - What tools worked?

3. **Network growth:**
   - Did I have real conversations (not just online)?
   - Did I move someone from online to in-person?
   - Did my pod meet?

4. **Power mapping:**
   - What new connections did I discover?
   - What leverage points became clearer?
   - What action could I take based on this?

**Plan next week (15 minutes):**

1. **One deep work block per day** - what will you actually work on?
2. **One contemplative session** - when exactly?
3. **One organizing conversation** - with whom?
4. **One power mapping entry** - which actor/connection?

**The format:**

Not bullet journal aesthetics.
Not productivity porn.
**Simple logging that shows patterns over time.**

Example:

```
Week 1 (Nov 4-10):
Deep work: 6 hours (goal: 10)
Yellow zone catches: 3
Pod meetings: 1
Power map: Added 4 actors, discovered council-developer connection

Week 2 plan:
Deep work: Housing policy research, 2hrs Mon/Wed/Fri
Regulation: Box breathing before team meetings
Organizing: Coffee with Discord member, invite to next pod
Power mapping: Trace developer's other projects
```

**What you're building:**

Not perfection.
Not optimization.
**Consistent marginal improvement over months.**

**Tomorrow's target:** You'll consolidate and commit.

**The tell:** "This isn't working for me."

**The pattern:** You do all the practices but they become routine without purpose. You're meditating and mapping but not building toward anything.

**The tell:** Your practices feel like chores, not capacity building.

**The pattern:** You build personal capacity (attention, regulation, mapping) but never move to organizing. Lone genius, no coalition.

**The tell:** You understand the system better but you're still acting alone.

**The fix:** Day 4 is non-negotiable. Without real-world network, you're just a well-regulated person watching the world burn.
