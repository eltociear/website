# Bioregional Mapping Guide
*Defining Governance Territories Through Traditional Ecological Knowledge and Natural Boundaries*

> *"The land is not something you own, it's something you belong to. The boundaries of true governance follow the paths of water, the migrations of our relatives, and the sacred places where our ancestors made covenant with the Earth."*  
> ‚Äî Elder Sarah James, Gwich'in Nation

## Tool Overview

The Bioregional Mapping Guide provides comprehensive methodology for defining governance territories based on ecological relationships, traditional territories, and natural systems rather than colonial administrative boundaries. This tool centers Traditional Ecological Knowledge while integrating contemporary mapping technologies to support Indigenous sovereignty and bioregional coordination.

**Essential for communities that are:**
- Establishing Bioregional Autonomous Zones (BAZs) based on natural boundaries
- Reclaiming traditional territorial governance and ecological stewardship
- Coordinating governance across watersheds and ecosystems
- Defining jurisdiction for community-controlled resource management
- Building inter-community coordination around shared ecological systems

**Traditional Knowledge Priority:** This mapping process is led by Traditional Ecological Knowledge, with contemporary technologies serving Indigenous knowledge rather than replacing it. All mapping respects Indigenous territorial sovereignty and sacred site protection protocols.

**FPIC Implementation Checklist:**
- [ ] **Free Consent**: No coercion, pressure, or manipulation in seeking territorial mapping consent
- [ ] **Prior Consent**: Consultation before any mapping activities begin or decisions are made
- [ ] **Informed Consent**: Complete information sharing about mapping purpose, methods, and potential impacts

- [ ] **Cultural Protocols**: Consent sought according to traditional decision-making timeframes and cultural processes
