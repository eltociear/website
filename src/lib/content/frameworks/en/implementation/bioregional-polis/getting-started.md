## <a id="know-your-territory"></a>First Steps: Know Your Territory

### Territorial Acknowledgment & Relationship

Before any implementation begins, **understand whose traditional territory you are on** and what relationships already exist between Indigenous communities and urban systems in your bioregion, including traditional astronomical knowledge and cosmic heritage.

**Research Traditional Territories**
- Identify Indigenous nations, tribes, and communities with historical and ongoing connections to your urban area
- Learn about traditional governance systems, ecological knowledge, and cultural protocols, including astronomical traditions
- Understand treaty relationships, land claims, and current sovereignty struggles
- Research sacred sites, traditional food systems, seasonal cycles, and traditional astronomical observation sites
- Learn about traditional star knowledge, constellation stories, and cosmic heritage belonging to Indigenous communities

**Assess Current Relationships**
- What existing partnerships exist between Indigenous communities and municipal governments?
- How are Indigenous communities currently represented (or excluded) in urban governance?
- What conflicts or tensions exist around land use, development, or cultural recognition, including cosmic heritage protection?
- What collaborative successes can be built upon?
- How are traditional astronomical sites and cosmic heritage currently protected or threatened?

**Honor Existing Leadership**
- Indigenous communities may already be leading urban transformation efforts, including dark sky preservation
- Traditional knowledge holders may have solutions you haven't considered, including astronomical wisdom for urban planning
- Youth from Indigenous communities may be organizing for change, including cosmic heritage protection
- Respect existing initiatives rather than starting competing efforts
- Recognize traditional astronomers and sky knowledge keepers as essential partners

### Bioregional Ecosystem Assessment

**Understand Your Watershed**
- Map the rivers, streams, and water systems flowing through your urban area
- Learn about traditional and current relationships between the city and surrounding ecosystems
- Identify upstream and downstream communities affected by urban activities
- Research seasonal patterns, wildlife corridors, and migration routes
- Understand how light pollution affects nocturnal wildlife and ecosystem health

**Assess Ecological Health**
- Current biodiversity levels and native species populations, including nocturnal species
- Air and water quality challenges and improvement opportunities  
- Urban heat island effects and climate vulnerability
- Existing green infrastructure and restoration potential
- Light pollution levels and impact on local ecosystems and human health

**Map Food & Resource Flows**
- Where does your city's food, energy, and materials come from?
- What are the social and ecological impacts of these supply chains?
- What local and regional production capacity exists or could be developed?
- How do resource flows affect Indigenous territories and communities?
- How much energy is wasted on unnecessary lighting, and what are the opportunities for efficiency?

### Cultural & Social Landscape Mapping

**Understand Community Composition**
- Who lives in your city and what communities do they belong to?
- What languages are spoken and what cultural traditions are practiced?
- How do different communities experience safety, accessibility, and inclusion?
- What are the current patterns of wealth, power, and resource distribution?
- How do different communities relate to darkness, lighting, and astronomical phenomena?

**Identify Existing Movements & Organizations**
- What community organizations are already working for justice and sustainability?
- Which groups are led by Indigenous people, people of color, disabled people, and other marginalized communities?
- What mutual aid networks, cooperatives, and alternative economy initiatives exist?
- How can you support rather than compete with existing efforts?
- Are there existing dark sky or astronomical organizations working in your area?

**Assess Governance Readiness**
- How open are current political structures to community participation?
- What legal and policy barriers exist to implementation?
- Where are there existing mechanisms for citizen engagement and democracy?
- What opposition might you face and from which interests?
- How do current lighting policies and practices reflect community priorities?

### Sky Quality & Cosmic Heritage Assessment

**Measure Current Sky Conditions**
- Document current light pollution levels using sky quality meters or smartphone apps
- Count visible stars and constellations from different neighborhood locations
- Identify major sources of light pollution and unnecessary lighting
- Map areas with better or worse sky quality across the city
- Document how sky visibility varies by season and weather conditions

**Research Astronomical Heritage**
- Learn about traditional astronomical knowledge and star stories from local Indigenous communities
- Identify historical astronomical observation sites and their current status
- Research any existing community interest in astronomy or stargazing
- Understand cultural and spiritual relationships with cosmic phenomena among diverse communities
- Assess existing astronomical education resources and institutions

**Document Community Cosmic Connection**
- Survey community members about their experiences with night skies and darkness
- Identify barriers to astronomical observation and cosmic connection
- Document how light pollution affects different community members (sleep, safety, cultural practices)
- Assess community interest in dark sky preservation and astronomical education
- Research connections between cosmic heritage and community identity
