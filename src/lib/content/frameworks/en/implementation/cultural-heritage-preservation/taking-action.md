# Taking action

[Back to Framework Overview](/frameworks/cultural-heritage-preservation)

## An invitation to build together

This framework was developed through collaboration between human vision and AI assistance to address a critical planetary challenge: **how do we prevent the irreversible loss of humanity's cultural heritage while ensuring communities maintain sovereignty over their own traditions?** 

The work you've just read represents deep research, careful design, and genuine hope—but it's currently a **vision seeking people to bring it to life**.

**The current reality**: This is a grassroots project, not (yet) a formal organization with funding, staff, or political backing. I'm one person working on the broader Global Governance Framework ecosystem, of which cultural heritage preservation is a vital component. These frameworks exist as carefully-researched blueprints awaiting the people, communities, and organizations who can transform vision into reality.

**The possibility**: The groundwork is substantial. These frameworks are being released openly, documented thoroughly, and designed for practical implementation. They're built on proven concepts (FPIC, Indigenous sovereignty, community-led development, participatory economics) while proposing innovative mechanisms (Living Heritage Licenses, Cultural AI Sandboxes, Generational Knowledge Banks) that could genuinely work.

**The invitation**: If you're reading this, you're encountering something rare—a comprehensive framework for planetary-scale cultural preservation that **prioritizes Indigenous sovereignty, maintains cultural integrity, enables economic sustainability, and provides concrete implementation pathways**. The question is: what role might you play in bringing it to life?

## Why this matters: The stakes

**The crisis is real**:
- One Indigenous language disappears every two weeks
- Museums hold millions of looted artifacts separated from their communities
- Climate change threatens irreplaceable cultural sites
- Digital appropriation enables cultural theft at unprecedented scale
- Communities face economic pressure to commodify traditions for survival

**The opportunity is genuine**:
- Traditional knowledge holds solutions to contemporary challenges
- Technology can serve sovereignty when community-controlled
- New economic models can value cultural preservation
- Global coordination can support rather than undermine local autonomy
- We have the knowledge—we need the will and the people

**The window is limited**:
- Languages are dying faster than they're being documented
- Elders holding irreplaceable knowledge are aging
- Climate impacts are accelerating
- Once lost, cultural knowledge cannot be recovered

This isn't fearmongering—it's reality. And it means **action taken now has outsized impact** compared to action delayed.

### For researchers and academics

Academic engagement can help or harm cultural preservation depending on approach:

**Helpful engagement**:
- Participatory action research led by communities
- Documentation that serves community priorities (not just academic careers)
- Research relationships based on FPIC 2.0 and genuine partnership
- Knowledge that returns to communities in usable forms
- Advocacy using academic credibility to support Indigenous rights

**Harmful engagement**:
- Extractive research that takes knowledge without community benefit
- Publications that violate cultural protocols about sacred knowledge
- "Studying" communities rather than supporting their priorities
- Using communities to advance academic careers without reciprocity

**Questions to ask yourself**:
- Does my research serve the community or my CV?
- Have I used genuine FPIC 2.0 consent processes?
- Will communities benefit from this knowledge production?
- Am I respecting cultural protocols about what can be shared?

**Ways to help**:
- Conduct community-requested research on preservation approaches
- Provide legal scholarship supporting Indigenous cultural rights
- Economic modeling of Heritage Enterprise viability
- Technology governance research for Cultural AI Sandboxes
- Always with community partnership, never extraction

### For policy makers and government officials

Government support or opposition will significantly impact implementation:

**How governments can help**:
- Ratify the Treaty for Our Only Home
- Recognize BAZ sovereignty over cultural heritage
- Enact strong cultural heritage protection laws
- Provide P4 contributions to global cultural preservation funding
- Remove legal barriers to Indigenous cultural governance
- Stop prosecuting traditional cultural practices

**How governments can harm** (don't do these things):
- Claim sovereignty over Indigenous heritage
- Block repatriation of looted artifacts
- Criminalize traditional practices
- Appropriate traditional knowledge for economic gain
- Undermine FPIC 2.0 with token consultation
- Defund Indigenous cultural programs

**Political reality check**: Many governments will resist genuine Indigenous sovereignty. The framework is designed to work around government obstruction when necessary, but government partnership would accelerate implementation significantly.

**For progressive officials**: If you hold office and want to support cultural preservation, focus on removing barriers rather than creating new programs. Often the best thing government can do is **get out of the way** while providing funding and legal recognition.

### For educators and cultural institutions

Museums, universities, libraries, and cultural organizations play complex roles:

**Museums holding looted heritage**:
- Proactively identify and return stolen artifacts
- Develop co-stewardship agreements with source communities
- Hire Indigenous curators and give them genuine authority
- Acknowledge colonial history and commit to repatriation
- Support FPIC 2.0 for any cultural exhibitions

**Universities and schools**:
- Teach accurate cultural history, not colonial myths
- Support Indigenous language programs
- Employ Indigenous scholars with tenure protection
- Respect traditional knowledge IP and protocols
- Create pathways for traditional knowledge holders to teach without credentials requirements

**Libraries and archives**:
- Digitize cultural materials and return copies to communities
- Respect cultural protocols about restricted knowledge
- Provide free access to cultural documentation
- Support community-controlled archiving initiatives

**Questions for self-reflection**:
- Does our institution hold stolen cultural property?
- Do we employ Indigenous people in leadership, not just consultation?
- Are we extracting cultural knowledge for institutional benefit?
- Would communities say we're helping or harming preservation?

### For artists, storytellers, and media makers

Cultural narratives shape public understanding and political possibilities:

**How to help**:
- Amplify Indigenous voices telling their own stories
- Challenge cultural appropriation and misrepresentation
- Document cultural preservation successes
- Create media showing possibility of different futures
- Use platforms to advocate for cultural rights

**How to harm**:
- Appropriate cultural expressions without permission or payment
- Tell Indigenous stories without Indigenous voices
- Exoticize or romanticize cultures
- Profit from cultural expression without community benefit
- Ignore FPIC 2.0 when documenting cultural practices

**Ethical creation checklist**:
- Have I used FPIC 2.0 consent processes?
- Are communities compensated fairly?
- Do communities control how they're represented?
- Am I supporting or extracting from cultural preservation?
- Would the communities I'm depicting approve of this work?

### For students and young people

**This is your future.** The cultural heritage we preserve or lose now shapes the world you'll inherit. Young people often see possibilities elders miss, bring digital native skills, and have most at stake in long-term outcomes.

**Ways to engage**:
- Learn about Indigenous cultures in your region (directly from Indigenous people)
- Support Indigenous-led cultural movements
- Challenge appropriation and misrepresentation when you see it
- Study fields enabling cultural preservation (conservation, linguistics, law, technology)
- Connect with youth cultural preservation networks
- Amplify Indigenous voices on social media

**Education pathways**:
- Indigenous studies (from Indigenous-led programs)
- Heritage conservation and preservation
- Linguistics and language revitalization
- Cultural anthropology (with decolonial approaches)
- Law (Indigenous rights, cultural IP, international law)

**The framework explicitly prioritizes youth**:

- Cultural Innovation Labs focus on contemporary creation
- Micro-grants for youth cultural workers

## Building the formal organization: A path forward

For this framework to reach its full potential, it needs to become a formal organization with:

**Governance**: Board with 60%+ Indigenous representation, clear accountability mechanisms, sunset clauses preventing capture

**Staff**: Small core team providing coordination, with most work done by BAZs and Cultural Stewardship Teams

**Funding**: Stewardship Bonds, philanthropic support, government contributions, eventually Heritage Enterprise revenue

**Legal status**: International nonprofit or similar entity capable of managing resources transparently

**Infrastructure**: Website, communication systems, technology platforms, financial management

**This is the opportunity**: If you have expertise in nonprofit formation, international governance, Indigenous rights law, or organizational development—and genuine commitment to Indigenous sovereignty—**your help building the formal structure could be transformative**.

This isn't about creating another colonial institution. It's about creating infrastructure that **serves communities while they maintain complete sovereignty over cultural decisions**.

## The question for you

After reading this framework, you have a choice:

**Dismiss it** as utopian dreaming disconnected from political reality. Fair enough—large-scale transformation is hard, and cynicism is often justified.

**Appreciate it** intellectually but take no action. Understandable—everyone's busy, and one more framework among thousands isn't necessarily worth your time.

**Engage with it** as a genuine possibility worth exploring. Find what resonates, adapt what's useful, critique what's problematic, and take whatever action fits your capacity—whether that's sharing it with one person or dedicating your career to implementation.

The choice is yours. Cultural heritage preservation is everyone's responsibility, but **Indigenous communities must lead**. Everyone else's role is supporting that leadership in ways that genuinely serve sovereignty rather than undermining it.

**The cultural heritage we save or lose in the next decade shapes humanity's future for millennia.** The question is whether we'll act with the urgency and integrity the moment demands.

If you're ready to help, we're ready to work together.
