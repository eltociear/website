Let's break down how conformity might manifest in this field and how you can consciously design to avoid its pitfalls.

### Potential Conformist Traps in Global Governance

1.  **The "Westphalian Sovereignty" Default:**
    *   **The Conformity:** The modern international system is built on the 1648 model of the nation-state as the primary, sovereign, and indivisible actor. This is so deeply ingrained that it's often treated as the *only* way to organize global politics.
    *   **The Autonomous Question:** Is this still the most effective model for handling transnational issues like climate change, pandemics, financial regulation, and cyber warfare? What governance structures could exist *alongside* or *above* the nation-state without merely replicating its limitations?

2.  **Economic Orthodoxy:**
    *   **The Conformity:** A deep-seated belief in neoliberal economics as the only path to development and stability. This includes faith in free markets, deregulation, and specific institutions (IMF, World Bank) as the primary arbiters of economic policy.
    *   **The Autonomous Question:** What economic models are we ignoring or suppressing? How can frameworks incorporate pluralistic economic thinking (e.g., doughnut economics, wellbeing economies, circular economies) that challenge this orthodoxy?

3.  **The "Expert" Class and Epistemic Conformity:**
    *   **The Conformity:** Governance is often left to a specific class of experts—economists, lawyers, and diplomats from a handful of elite institutions. This creates a "groupthink" on what constitutes valid knowledge, data, and solutions.
    *   **The Autonomous Question:** How can frameworks systematically incorporate indigenous knowledge, local community wisdom, and perspectives from outside the traditional "expert" bubble? Who is considered a "stakeholder" and why?

4.  **Legalistic and Bureaucratic Inertia:**
    *   **The Conformity:** The belief that complex global problems can be solved by writing more detailed laws, regulations, and non-binding agreements, often following pre-existing templates. This is a mechanical, "aping" process.
    *   **The Autonomous Question:** Are we prioritizing the *act of governing* (creating documents) over the *outcome of governance* (solving problems)? How can we build frameworks that are adaptive, learning-oriented, and outcome-based rather than just procedural?

5.  **"Wokeness" and "Anti-Wokeness" in Development:**
    *   **The Conformity:** As Leo pointed out, both sides can be conformist. This manifests in blindly adopting certain buzzwords (e.g., "decolonization," "sustainability," "resilience") as signaling virtues without deep, critical implementation, or on the flip side, reflexively rejecting them without consideration.
    *   **The Autonomous Question:** Are we using these terms because they are truly insightful and operationalizable, or because they are the required password to access funding and approval within our professional tribe?

### Strategies for Designing Non-Conformist Governance Frameworks

To build frameworks that resist mechanical conformity and embody "autonomous mind," you could bake in the following principles:

1.  **Build in Mandatory Challenge Mechanisms:**
    *   Institutionalize "Red Teams" or "Devil's Advocate" roles whose sole job is to attack and find flaws in proposed frameworks.
    *   Create sunset clauses for every major regulation and agreement, forcing periodic re-evaluation rather than letting them persist on autopilot.

2.  **Design for Cognitive Diversity:**
    *   **Explicitly require** the inclusion of members with radically different backgrounds: not just different countries, but different disciplines (artists, philosophers, psychologists, ecologists) and different lived experiences.
    *   Structure deliberation to ensure these minority voices are heard and cannot be easily dismissed by the conformist majority.

3.  **Focus on Meta-Governance:**
    *   Don't just design a framework for a specific problem (e.g., climate). Design a framework for *how to design frameworks*. This means creating processes that themselves combat groupthink and encourage independent inquiry.
    *   Example: A "Principle of First Principles" that requires every proposal to state its fundamental assumptions upfront for examination.

4.  **Embrace Pluralism and Experimentation:**
    *   Reject the idea of a single, monolithic global framework for complex problems. Instead, design for "pluralateral" agreements or encourage a "marketplace of governance models" where cities, regions, and willing nations can experiment with different approaches. The best solutions can then be observed and adopted, not dictated.

5.  **Integrate Contemplative Practices:**
    *   This is a radical but crucial one. Borrowing from Leo's core work, you could design processes that include moments for silent reflection, mindfulness, or other techniques that allow participants to step out of their programmed identities and biases and connect with a more fundamental sense of shared humanity and purpose.

By applying this lens, you're not just tweaking policies; you are engaging in a form of **"epistemic governance"**—governing the very ways of knowing and thinking that underpin our systems. This is arguably the highest leverage point for creating meaningful change.

Your work has the potential to be a practical embodiment of the transition from a "conformist" to an "autonomous" stage of development at a civilizational scale. That's a profoundly important undertaking.
